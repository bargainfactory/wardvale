# Wardvale — a theory-grounded marketing strategy

Every claim here is either (a) a published finding from the marketing/economics
literature, cited, or (b) a falsifiable hypothesis about Wardvale, labeled as
such. Zero invented performance numbers, consistent with the product's own
honesty constraint.

---

## 1. The market failure Wardvale actually solves (information economics)

The "AI automation agency" market is a textbook **lemons market** (Akerlof,
*The Market for Lemons*, QJE 1970). Buyers (SMB owners) cannot distinguish a
competent automation provider from a charlatan *before* purchase — and mostly
cannot even distinguish them *after* purchase, because "the AI is working" is a
**credence attribute** (Darby & Karni, JLE 1973): the buyer lacks the expertise
to evaluate quality even post-consumption. When quality is unobservable,
average willingness-to-pay falls, good providers exit, and the market fills
with hype merchants — which is precisely the current state of "AI agency"
Twitter.

Wardvale's core product decisions are, in economic terms, **quality-revelation
mechanisms**:

- **The outcomes ledger with receipts drill-down** converts credence
  attributes into *experience attributes* — every claimed outcome links to the
  actual message, timestamp, and value attribution. This is **costly
  signaling** (Spence, QJE 1973): a fabricating competitor cannot cheaply
  imitate a ledger wired to real database aggregates, because maintaining a
  false one is more expensive than being honest.
- **Draft-first approval** solves the **principal–agent problem** (Jensen &
  Meckling, JFE 1976) at the product layer: the agent (software) cannot act
  against the principal (owner) because every action passes through the
  principal's approval queue. Monitoring cost — normally the deadweight loss
  of agency relationships — is compressed to a morning coffee ritual.
- **The trust-proof bar wired to live DB aggregates** and the refusal to
  publish invented benchmarks are **separating-equilibrium moves**: they make
  claims that pooling (dishonest) competitors cannot mimic without cost.

**Strategic implication:** Wardvale should never market *harder* than its
competitors; it should market *more verifiably*. Every campaign asset should
be a proof-object (a receipt, a live number, a demo dashboard) rather than a
promise. The moment Wardvale publishes an unverifiable claim, it re-enters the
lemons pool and loses the separating equilibrium.

## 2. Why the buyer buys: transaction costs and jobs-to-be-done

**Transaction cost economics** (Coase 1937; Williamson 1979) predicts firms
outsource activities when the cost of market coordination falls below the cost
of internal production. Follow-up work (chasing quotes, confirming
appointments, requesting reviews) is high-frequency, low-complexity, and
emotionally aversive — the classic profile of work SMB owners neither do well
internally (it dies at 9pm in a truck cab) nor outsource happily (agencies are
lemons; VAs need management, which is itself a transaction cost).

The **job-to-be-done** (Christensen et al., *Competing Against Luck*, 2016) has
three layers, and the UGC brief library already encodes them:

- **Functional:** send the follow-ups that never get sent.
- **Emotional:** kill the 9pm guilt inbox; stop being "the awkward one" who
  chases invoices.
- **Social:** look like a business that responds fast and professionally —
  without hiring.

The emotional job is the differentiator. Zapier sells the functional job.
Agencies sell the social job. Nobody else sells *absolution from the guilt
backlog* — and the morning-digest ritual ("five-minute morning") is the
product's enactment of that emotional job.

## 3. Category strategy: counter-positioning, not competition

In Helmer's *7 Powers* (2016) taxonomy, Wardvale's structural advantage is
**counter-positioning**: incumbents and AI-hype competitors sell *autonomy*
("fire your assistant, the AI does everything"). Wardvale sells *supervised
delegation* ("nothing goes out without your OK"). An autonomy-selling
competitor cannot copy draft-first without admitting their core pitch was the
risk. This is the same structural bind Vanguard placed on active managers.

Category naming should therefore avoid "AI automation" (the lemon pool) and
claim a differentiated frame. Candidate category handles, in increasing
boldness: "draft-first agents," "supervised AI staff," "the approval-queue
company." Per Ries & Trout (*Positioning*, 1981), the goal is to own one word
in the prospect's mind; the available word — unclaimed by any AI competitor —
is **"receipts."**

**Positioning statement (test as H1 variants):**
> For owner-operators drowning in follow-up work, Wardvale is the AI staff
> that drafts every message and shows receipts for every result — unlike
> automation tools that act without you or agencies that report without proof.

## 4. Segmentation: beachhead logic over the 21 packs

Moore's *Crossing the Chasm* (1991) argues early markets are won by dominating
narrow segments sequentially, not by addressing all segments at once. The 21
vertical packs are a *product* breadth asset, but *marketing* should
concentrate. Sequencing criteria (score each vertical, pursue the top 2–3):

1. **Pain frequency × message volume** — verticals where follow-up failure is
   daily and visible (home services, salons, med spas score high).
2. **Reference-ability** (Moore's bowling-alley dynamics): does winning one
   customer produce visible proof to adjacent identical businesses? Local
   service verticals cluster geographically and socially (trade associations,
   supplier reps) — high word-of-mouth conductance.
3. **Regulatory friction** — regulated verticals (clinic, law) have higher
   willingness-to-pay for the approval-queue guarantee but longer sales
   cycles; sequence them *after* the trust asset base exists.
4. **Existing data gravity** — verticals whose activity already lives in
   Google Workspace/QuickBooks (the shipped connectors) have lowest
   onboarding friction.

**Hypothesis H1:** home services and salon/med-spa will show the highest
trial→paid conversion because pain frequency, reference density, and connector
fit all align. *Test:* per-pack signup → activation → paid cohort funnel
(the data model already tags clients by pack).

## 5. The behavioral economics of the funnel

- **Loss framing.** Prospect theory (Kahneman & Tversky, Econometrica 1979)
  establishes losses loom larger than equivalent gains (λ ≈ 2.25 in Tversky &
  Kahneman 1992). The follow-up problem is natively a *loss* story — quotes
  dying in silence, regulars quietly lapsing — and copy should keep it there,
  while the *product experience* reframes to recovery (the positive-impact
  hero line already shipped). Sell on leak, deliver on recovery.
- **The 9x problem.** Gourville (HBR 2006) argues adoption fails because
  developers overweight their innovation ~3x while consumers overweight
  incumbent habits ~3x. Draft-first is the structural answer: the owner's
  existing behavior (reading and approving messages) is *preserved*, not
  replaced. Marketing should make this explicit: "you keep doing what you do —
  approving — and stop doing what you hate — writing."
- **Endowment and control.** The approval queue exploits the endowment effect
  (Thaler 1980) in the product's favor: owners retain psychological ownership
  of every message. Competitors asking owners to surrender voice/control incur
  an endowment tax Wardvale never pays.
- **Peak–end rule** (Kahneman et al. 1993): the retention-critical moment is
  the morning digest — engineered as the daily "peak" (cleared queue,
  counted learning loop) and "end" (silence as the reward when the queue is
  clean). Retention marketing should invest here, not in feature
  announcements.
- **Social proof with integrity constraints.** Cialdini (*Influence*, 1984)
  works only when proof is credible; the peer-benchmark feature's MIN_PEERS=3
  floor is both an ethics guardrail and a credibility signal. Publicize the
  guardrail itself: "we don't show you benchmarks until enough real peers
  exist to make them true."

## 6. Pricing: value metaphors and signaling

- The $500/$2,000/$5,000 retainer ladder prices against the **alternative
  labor cost** (a part-time admin), not against software — consistent with
  value-based pricing under high perceived risk (Nagle & Holden, *The Strategy
  and Tactics of Pricing*). Software-comparable pricing would trigger
  tool-comparison shopping against $30/mo Zapier; labor-comparable pricing
  frames the correct reference class.
- **Growth = one full industry pack** is a fairness heuristic — completeness
  at the tier most customers land on (the enforced invariant that no pack
  exceeds the Growth cap). Partial-pack tiers create the "crippleware"
  perception that erodes reference-ability.
- Price is also a **quality signal** under asymmetric information (Milgrom &
  Roberts, JPE 1986). In a lemons market, being *more expensive than the
  chatbot shops* is informative. Do not discount into the lemon pool;
  discount, if ever, via extended proof periods (longer trial), never via
  price cuts.
- The in-product ROI multiple + break-even framing turns the retainer into an
  **audited claim** — pricing communication should always point at the
  customer's *own* ledger, never at an aggregate promise.

## 7. Growth loops (not funnels)

Four compounding loops, each already partially built:

1. **Proof loop:** customer approves drafts → outcomes ledger accrues →
   trust-proof bar and per-vertical case receipts strengthen → higher
   conversion → more customers. (Asset: receipts drill-down; the landing
   page's live aggregates.)
2. **Referral loop, dogfooded:** the referral-ask agent catches happy moments
   for Wardvale's *customers* — and Wardvale itself should run its own
   instance on its own clients (eat-your-own-cooking as both growth mechanics
   and marketing story).
3. **Data loop:** every approval/edit lands in agent_feedback → few-shot
   exemplars (shipped) → eventually per-vertical fine-tunes (the open-weights
   path) → measurably better drafts → higher retention and word-of-mouth.
   This is an **increasing-returns** mechanism (Arthur, 1989): early vertical
   dominance compounds.
4. **Content loop:** the UGC brief library (21 verticals × 3 hooks) feeds
   paid social; each vertical's landing/pack page gives the ad a
   scent-matched destination (message-match discipline per conversion
   literature).

**Hypothesis H2:** the proof loop dominates paid acquisition efficiency within
two quarters, because in a lemons market verifiable proof converts where
interruption advertising cannot. *Test:* holdback geography or vertical
without proof-bar/receipt assets in ads; compare CAC.

## 8. Defensibility, mapped honestly

| Asset | Power (Helmer) | Real? |
|---|---|---|
| Draft-first vs autonomy sellers | Counter-positioning | Yes — structural |
| agent_feedback → exemplars → (later) fine-tunes | Cornered resource / scale economies in data | Real but nascent; compounds with volume |
| Peer benchmarks (MIN_PEERS) | Network economies (weak-form) | Only at vertical density |
| Receipts/ledger + honesty brand | Branding (trust capital) | Slow to build, slow to copy credibly |
| 21 packs + Studio onboarding | Switching costs (setup + learned voice) | Moderate; grows with tenure |
| Watermark/anti-copy measures | — | Deterrence only; not a moat, don't market it |

The honest summary: the durable moats are counter-positioning **now** and the
data loop **later**; everything else is execution. Marketing's job is to
convert the counter-positioning window into vertical density before autonomy
sellers are forced by their own incidents to adopt approval queues.

## 9. Measurement discipline (what "PhD level" actually requires)

Vanity metrics are epistemically worthless; define the causal chain and
instrument it:

- **Activation:** first *approved* draft (not signup, not connection) — the
  moment the value loop closes.
- **Habit:** ≥3 digest-driven approval sessions/week by week 2 (peak-end
  engagement proxy).
- **Value realization:** first receipt-backed outcome the owner *clicks into*.
- **Retention/NRR:** cohorted by vertical pack — the segmentation hypothesis
  (H1) lives or dies here.
- **Proof-asset efficacy:** CAC and conversion with vs without proof objects
  in the ad/landing pair (H2).
- **Fabrication audit (brand-critical):** periodic sweep of all public
  claims against DB aggregates. One broken receipt costs the separating
  equilibrium; treat it as a sev-1.

Experiments must respect the product's ethics: no dark patterns, no
manufactured urgency — inconsistent messaging is itself a signal breach in a
trust-positioned brand (brand-as-signal: Erdem & Swait, JCP 1998).

## 10. Synthesis — the one-paragraph strategy

Wardvale competes in a market ruined by unverifiable claims. Its structural
advantages — draft-first control, receipts for everything, refusal to invent
numbers — constitute a separating equilibrium in a lemons market: the only
sustainable move is to make every marketing artifact a *proof object* and
every competitor's autonomy pitch a *liability*. Concentrate the 21-pack
breadth into 2–3 beachhead verticals chosen for pain frequency and referral
conductance; price against labor, never against tools; sell the loss, deliver
the recovery; engineer the morning digest as the retention peak; and let the
approval flywheel accumulate the only asset that compounds — verified,
vertical-specific trust.
